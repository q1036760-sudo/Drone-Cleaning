The Challenge

The Problem with Traditional High-Rise Cleaning

Let us be direct. Scaffolding-based and rope-access cleaning is dangerous, slow, expensive, and disruptive. In markets where labour is relatively cheap — like much of Latin America — the cost argument is sometimes used to justify continuing the old way. But cost per hour of labour is only one variable in the equation. Here is what the full picture actually looks like:

  • Safety liability: Every worker deployed at height is a liability. Near-misses are underreported. Fatalities are not unheard of. The human cost alone should be reason enough to look for alternatives.
  • Speed: A traditional scaffolding setup for a mid-rise commercial building can take one to two days before cleaning even begins. A drone operation is airborne within minutes.
  • Disruption: Scaffolding blocks entrances, obstructs signage, and creates noise and chaos for tenants, customers, and visitors — a reputational and operational problem for hotels, retail centres, and offices alike.
  • Chemical residue: Traditional cleaning chemicals leave toxic residues on surfaces, in drainage systems, and in the air around occupied buildings — a growing concern for ESG-conscious property owners.
  • Frequency: Because traditional cleaning is so costly and disruptive, most buildings are cleaned far less often than they should be — accelerating facade deterioration over time.

In a region like Latin America, where commercial real estate is expanding rapidly — particularly in markets like El Salvador, which is experiencing one of its fastest periods of economic and business growth in decades — the pressure to maintain building appearance and operational efficiency is intensifying. The old methods are simply not built for this pace.

Our Technology

What Is Ionized Water Drone Cleaning — and Why Does It Work?

When most people hear "drone cleaning," they picture a consumer quadcopter with a garden hose taped to it. The reality is considerably more sophisticated — and the results speak for themselves.

Our system uses a professional-grade, tethered heavy-lift drone equipped with a precision nozzle and an integrated ionized water delivery system. The drone is operated by a single trained technician on the ground. There is no one on the facade. There is no rope. There is no scaffold. The only thing touching your building is water.

Why ionized water specifically?

Ionized water — sometimes called electrolyzed water — is water that has been treated to alter its electrical charge. This process gives it properties that ordinary water simply does not have:

  • Cuts through grease and grime at a molecular level, without the need for harsh chemical detergents.
  • Disinfects surfaces — ionized water is clinically proven to neutralize bacteria and pathogens on contact, making it particularly valuable for healthcare facilities, hotels, and food-adjacent commercial properties.
  • Leaves zero toxic residue — it reverts to ordinary water after application, meaning no chemical runoff into drainage systems and no hazard to people in or around the building.
  • Delivers a streak-free, fast-drying finish — the molecular structure of ionized water means it sheets off glass cleanly, without the streaking that soap-based systems commonly produce.

The first time a building owner sees this in action, the reaction is almost always the same: shock, followed by amazement. Because it is genuinely something most people have never seen. A drone hovering in front of a glass facade, spraying a precise jet of water, leaving clean panels behind it — it looks like something from the future. In Latin America right now, it essentially is.

The Concern

"Is it actually safe? What if the drone crashes into the building?"

The Reality

Our drone systems use real-time obstacle detection and automated failsafe protocols. The tethered system keeps the drone within a controlled operational envelope. Critically, no human is ever at height — eliminating the single biggest safety risk of traditional cleaning entirely.

The Concern

"What if it breaks a window or damages the facade?"

The Reality

The ionized water system uses a precision nozzle calibrated for the specific surface type. Water pressure is carefully controlled. Every operation is covered by full liability insurance. In fact, chemical-free water is significantly less damaging to seals and frames than traditional solvent-based cleaning over time.

Ionized water cuts through grease, neutralizes bacteria, and leaves a streak-free finish. The before-and-after results are visible and immediate. This is not a novelty — it is a proven industrial cleaning method, now delivered at height without scaffolding.

Market Opportunity

Why Latin America — and Why Now

This is where it gets interesting. Drone cleaning is already established in parts of Asia, the United States, and Europe. In Latin America, it is almost entirely untapped. That is not a weakness — it is a window.

The argument that cheap labour makes drone cleaning unnecessary misses the bigger picture. Yes, manual labour costs less per hour in Mexico or El Salvador than in Germany or California. But that calculation ignores several critical factors:

  • The total operational cost — including scaffolding hire, insurance, disruption to tenants, and time lost.
  • The liability exposure — one serious injury on a job site can cost a building owner far more than years of drone cleaning contracts.
  • The appearance economy — as Latin American cities grow and attract international investment, the standard for commercial property presentation is rising rapidly. A dirty building is no longer acceptable in competitive markets.
  • The sustainability pressure — ESG requirements are reaching Latin American commercial real estate. Chemical-free, low-disruption maintenance is no longer a luxury positioning — it is becoming an expectation.

El Salvador is a particularly compelling example. The country is experiencing a period of rapid economic transformation — new commercial developments, international business investment, and a growing hospitality and retail sector. Businesses in this environment want to look the part. Curb appeal is not vanity — it is a competitive strategy.

The opportunity in Latin America right now is comparable to what solar energy was fifteen years ago — a technology that works, a market that is ready, and very few operators yet established. The brands that move first will define the category.
